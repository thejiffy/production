# Episode 1: *Upstate, Where?*

## A Full Pre-Production Map

---

## THE CORE EDITORIAL PREMISE

This episode is asking a deceptively simple question — and that's exactly what makes it great radio. "Where is upstate New York?" sounds like a geography question, but it's actually a question about **identity, belonging, class, and who gets to define a place**. The strongest version of this episode doesn't just answer the question — it dramatizes the *disagreement itself* as the story.

The through-line: **The boundary doesn't exist because upstate isn't a place — it's a feeling. And the fight over where it starts tells you everything about what people think it means.**

---

## STRUCTURAL APPROACH

Think in three movements:

### Movement 1 — The Question from Ward's Point on Staten Island

You, in the field, posing the question casually and letting it unravel. Open with a vox-pop montage: you asking strangers on the street (maybe in Kingston, New Paltz, or Poughkeepsie — the contested zone) *"Are you in upstate New York right now?"* Let the disagreement be the first thing the listener hears. No music yet. Just voices and ambient street sound.

### Movement 2 — The Lines People Draw

This is where you explore the competing definitions:

- The I-84 / Newburgh line (popular folk boundary)

- The Stewart's Shops boundary (your instinct here is perfect — more below)

- New York State legal definitions (multiple, contradicting statutes)

- The NYC Metro area as the implicit "not upstate"

- Newspaper/outlet coverage zones

- The "North of the City" instinct vs. the Hudson Valley's identity crisis

### Movement 3 — What the Boundary Is Really About

Bring in the cultural historians and geographers. Reframe: the line isn't geographic — it's psychological. Who claims upstate identity and why? What does calling something "downstate" do politically? End with you returning to one of your field locations, letting the ambiguity breathe.

### Ending — at Barnhardt Island in Massena, New York

Closing: Welcome to season 2 of The Jiffy -- a podcast about... well, upstate New York, whatever that means.

## FIELD RECORDING LOCATIONS

### 🏁 Priority 1: The Southernmost Stewart's

Your instinct here is exactly right — and it's great radio because it's *specific and weird*. A gas station chain as a cultural boundary marker is a perfect *Jiffy* moment.

**Production note:** Record the ambient environment — pumps, the coffee counter, the parking lot. Talk to the cashier and customers. Ask them: *"Are you in upstate New York right now?"* The Stewart's itself becomes a sonic postcard.

**Research task:** Confirm the current southernmost location. As of recent years, the southernmost Stewart's Shops locations have been in the **Newburgh/Wallkill area of Orange County** — around the I-84 corridor. Verify this before you go, as locations occasionally open/close.

### 🏁 Priority 2: The Contested Boundary Zone

Spend time in **Poughkeepsie or Kingston** — places that sit in the gray zone. These cities are claimed as both "the Hudson Valley" and "upstate" depending on who's talking. The acoustic environment of a main street there + vox pops from locals give you sonic texture and genuine disagreement.

### 🏁 Priority 3: A "Definitely Upstate" Anchor

Consider a brief scene from somewhere unambiguously upstate — **Oneonta, Utica, or the Catskill village of Delhi** — to give the listener an acoustic contrast. This is the "you know it when you see it" moment.

### Optional: A State Office Building or Courthouse

If you can get access, recording a brief ambient scene near a state government building (Albany?) where the contradicting legal definitions originate adds institutional texture.

## WHAT THE EPISODE IS NOT

Don't let this become a listicle with a narrator. Resist the urge to just tick through "here are 5 different definitions." The definitions should emerge from *people* and *places*, not from you reading bullet points into a mic. Every data point should come from a human voice or a location.
